Tonalign

Role: Designer & developer (with Brady Boettcher)

VST3 / AU / AAX

2024–present MIDI Plugin

Tonalign started because I wanted the MIDI equivalent of AutoTune — my first real plugin.

At the time I was building Scale Navigator as a web application. I knew almost nothing about JUCE or C++. I posted on the JUCE forum looking for help and met Brady Boettcher. This was mid-pandemic; he was often in a completely different time zone. Over the next few months we pair-programmed the first version together — Brady brought the C++ and JUCE expertise, I brought the design and the Dashboard integration concept. Watching him work fundamentally changed how I approached audio software.

What it does

AutoTune corrects audio pitch. Tonalign does the same thing for MIDI. You play a note that doesn't fit the current scale, and it snaps to the nearest note that does.

If Dashboard is running in the same session, Tonalign subscribes to its harmonic state. Change the scale in Dashboard and every instance of Tonalign updates instantly.

The hard part

MIDI pitch correction sounds trivial until you try to build it. The tricky cases are things like: what happens when you're holding a note and the scale changes underneath you? Do you re-pitch the held note (which sounds weird) or let it ring out of key (which also sounds weird)? I ended up implementing configurable behavior — you can choose whether held notes glide to the new pitch, cut off, or stay put.

Why it matters

The collaboration outlived the project — Brady has continued building on the ecosystem, most recently a scale-synchronized interface for the LinnStrument.

But for me, Tonalign was the first project that convinced me harmonic state could become shared infrastructure rather than just a visualization. It proved that Dashboard's scale data could leave the browser and drive another tool in real time. It now ships signed and notarized on Mac, and it's the quiet backbone that keeps the other Scale Navigator tools in tune.

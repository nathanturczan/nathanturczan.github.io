Scale Navigator Dashboard

Role: Product designer & developer (sole)

Web, iOS, Android, macOS/Windows, VST3/AU/AAX

2021–present Software

I'd be in a session, move from C major to something more interesting, and then have to manually update my synth, my sampler, my arpeggiator — each one independently. The harmony was in my head, but every tool needed to be told separately.

I wanted harmony to have one place to live.

The core interface is a network of scales where neighbors differ by only one note. Instead of wondering where to go next, you can see nearby possibilities.

Early feedback

The first prototype was a web app where you could click through the network. It worked as a way to explore, but it had a problem.

At the time I was contracting at Output, where Arthur Carabott led interface design. I cold-emailed him asking if he'd critique the prototype. He gave me two pieces of feedback that took years to fully address:

"Once I've found a chord I like, I want to be able to actually learn that, or save it. At the moment it is very easy to just keep clicking and lose what I had."

"It's the difference between someone demonstrating something on stage, vs a teacher helping you learn it."

The tool was good for wandering but bad for composing. You'd stumble onto something beautiful and then click again and lose it.

The sketchpad

It took years to get right, but I eventually built a sketchpad where you can save scale/chord pairs and arrange them into progressions. You explore the network, find something you like, and drag it in.

I emailed Arthur again in 2026 to tell him the feature finally existed. He'd moved to Splice by then and was writing a book on music UI.

Where harmony should live

Harmony should live at the center of the production session, not in a browser, not inside every plugin.

So I rebuilt Dashboard as a VST3/AU/AAX plugin with automatable parameters. Change the scale on bar 32, and the automation lane remembers. Every other Scale Navigator tool — Tonalign, SNaPS, Ensemble Jammer — subscribes to that state automatically.

Decisions I left as toggles

I tested both a 3D layout and a flat 2D layout. Users gravitate toward 3D — you're flying through a space. But 2D reveals the circle of fifths as just one pathway through a larger network. Both stay as options.

Similarly: early users expected clockwise rotation to mean flats. Dmitri Tymoczko — the music theorist whose research the whole project is based on (NIME 2019 paper) — preferred the opposite. I added a toggle.

I also shipped iOS and Android apps for exploration away from the computer.

Where it is now

Since launching on iOS in April 2026: 12% conversion rate (>75th percentile for music apps), 272 downloads. A UX professional on Reddit wrote "Exploring chords feels so natural and playful" and volunteered to beta test.

The thing I'm still working on is onboarding. First-time users need a guided path that teaches the network by having them use it, not read about it.
